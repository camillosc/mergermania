THE REAL HOUSEHOLD DSR

The Fed's published Household Debt Service Ratio (FRED TDSP) is built from bank balance sheets and public-securities data. It misses three categories — margin debt, BNPL, and the private-credit web that ultimately funds non-bank consumer paper. Add them back and the picture changes.

Why these three?

Per Pozsar, Adrian, Ashcraft & Boesky (2010), the post-1980 shift from a bank-intermediated to a market-intermediated credit system created a "shadow" footprint that the official statistics do not yet fully measure. The Board discontinued the Financial Obligations Ratio in 2023 citing data quality — the surviving DSR (TDSP) is built from mortgage and consumer-credit flows on bank books. Three categories of household-linked credit sit outside that frame: securities-collateralized margin loans (treated as asset-backed, not consumer credit), BNPL receivables (largely off bank balance sheets and outside the major bureaus), and the private-credit complex that increasingly funds non-bank consumer paper (BNPL forward-flow programs, fintech ABS, and direct lending to consumer-facing platforms).

The implication is not that the Fed is wrong — it's that the official series has a specific scope, and that scope has shrunk relative to the actual debt-service surface every year since roughly 2019.

01 Margin debt impact on household DSR

FINRA-reported margin debt, valued at retail broker rates

Margin loans are recorded in the Fed's Z.1 financial accounts as a household liability (security credit), but they are excluded from the DSR because they're asset-backed — the methodology treats them as a portfolio-allocation choice, not a debt-service obligation. That logic breaks down when prices fall: margin calls then translate the synthetic exposure directly into a liquidity demand. Adding them back at the prevailing broker-call rate (SOFR + ~600bps retail blend) is the simplest correction.

02 BNPL impact on household DSR

Aggregate BNPL receivables, blended-rate service cost

The Pay-in-4 product is the dominant BNPL form, charges 0% nominal interest, and turns over in 6 weeks — so outstanding balances are small relative to the ~$200B annual GMV. But the consumer's effective service cost is not zero: it is the weighted average of late-fee revenue and charge-offs on the interest-free book (per the CFPB BNPL Market Report, Dec 2025), plus the ~25–30% installment APRs on Affirm-style longer-duration loans. Blending those gives ~17% effective annual service cost applied to estimated aggregate outstanding receivables. Critically, almost none of this hits the Fed's primary data feeds: most BNPL providers do not furnish to the major credit bureaus, and their receivables sit on issuer or securitization balance sheets, not bank books.

COMPANION SECTION — CORPORATE COVERAGE

PRIVATE CREDIT & THE SHADOW INTEREST BURDEN

Private credit doesn't directly touch the household DSR — it touches the corporate coverage analogue. But it is the same shadow-banking phenomenon, and it deserves a parallel adjustment for anyone using Fed Z.1 aggregate interest-coverage ratios as a systemic indicator.

Private credit add-on to corporate interest aggregate

Private credit AUM (per Preqin Global Report 2026) is $2.28T as of YE 2025, projected to reach $4.5T by 2030. The book is overwhelmingly floating-rate at SOFR + 500–700bps, with maturities concentrated in middle-market direct loans, BDC portfolios, and securitized fund formats. None of this shows up in the public-bond interest aggregates that Z.1 captures for nonfinancial corporates, yet it is genuine interest expense on the corporate balance sheets that owe it. Adding it back gives a meaningfully different read on "shadow corporate coverage."
